There's a version of AI-assisted development that's genuinely dangerous: a junior developer fires prompts at an LLM, ships what comes out, and calls it done. The code works — until it doesn't. Until it hits production load, or a security audit, or someone needs to maintain it six months later.

That's not what I do.

Over the last few years I've built a development workflow where AI agents handle the mechanical parts of the job: generating boilerplate, scaffolding tests, catching obvious code smells, running through repetitive implementation patterns. This is real leverage. Work that used to take days now takes hours.

But here's what doesn't change: every architectural decision still goes through 15 years of enterprise experience.

## The part AI can't replace

An AI model doesn't know your system's history. It doesn't know why that design decision was made three years ago, or what the security implications are of this particular data flow, or which parts of the codebase will be touched by six other teams. It doesn't have opinions about maintainability that are grounded in having shipped real software at scale.

I do.

So when I work with AI agents, the division of labor is deliberate:

- **AI handles:** boilerplate, test generation, code review passes, refactoring patterns, documentation drafts
- **I handle:** system design, security posture, performance architecture, integration strategy, the decisions that will matter in two years

The output is enterprise-grade software. The timeline is aggressive. That's not a contradiction — it's the point.

## What this means for teams

This workflow isn't proprietary. It's teachable. Part of what I do is help development teams set up their own AI-augmented processes — tooling, prompting patterns, review checkpoints — so they get the same leverage without the same learning curve.

If your team is using AI tools but not systematically, you're getting some of the speed and most of the risk.

## The bottom line

AI development is not a shortcut. Used correctly, it's a force multiplier. The multiplier only pays off if the thing being multiplied is already good.

Fifteen years of building software that doesn't break: that's what's being multiplied.
